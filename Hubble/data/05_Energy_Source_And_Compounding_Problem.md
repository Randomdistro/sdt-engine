## 1. Matter Persists Without Running Down

Observed:
- Proton lifetime: > 10³⁴ years (no observed decay)
- Hydrogen atoms from early epochs: still present, same mass,
  same charge, same spectral lines (verified by absorption
  lines in high-z quasar spectra — same atomic physics at z=7
  as at z=0)
- No observed mass loss, size change, or structural degradation
  in any fundamental particle over any timescale

If matter is a topological structure in a medium (any medium —
lattice, field, spacetime fabric), maintaining that structure
is work. Work requires energy input. Three possibilities:

### Option A: Prewound, running down

Matter was created with a fixed energy budget and is slowly
depleting it. Eventually it will decay.

Problem: no evidence of any rundown. The proton at z=7 (when
the universe was ~800 Myr old in ΛCDM) has the same properties
as the proton at z=0 (13.8 Gyr later). Over 13 Gyr of
operation, zero measurable degradation.

### Option B: Shrinking at a flat rate

Matter converts rest mass to operational energy, losing mass
and shrinking over time. This would produce:
- A universal redshift (everything shrinks → emitters were
  larger in the past → wavelengths were longer → apparent
  redshift)
- An apparent acceleration (shrinkage is proportional to
  current size → exponential)

Problem: different matter would age at different rates.
Over 13+ Gyr, the size and velocity differences would be
stark. We do not observe this. All hydrogen atoms at all
redshifts have identical spectral signatures. No systematic
size evolution.

### Option C: Externally fueled

Matter is continuously sustained by an external energy source.
It doesn't need stored energy because the energy arrives
constantly from outside.

The source: the convergent throughput. The pressure gradient
from the CMB shell (the Clearing boundary) provides
omnidirectional pressure. Matter occludes some of this
pressure, creating a shadow (deficit) on one side. The
pressure differential across the object:
1. IS gravity (the shadow creates asymmetric force)
2. Sustains the topological structure (the pressure maintains
   the winding against its own tendency to unravel)

Matter doesn't run down because it's not running ON stored
energy. It's running on the gradient. As long as the gradient
exists, matter persists.

## 2. The Gradient Is Geometric

The convergent throughput originates from a spherical shell
(the Clearing boundary — the surface from which the CMB is
arriving). At any point in the interior, the throughput from
this shell provides isotropic pressure.

The intensity of this throughput at distance r from the shell
boundary follows from geometry:
- The shell emits isotropically inward
- At distance r from the boundary, the flux is spread over
  a sphere of radius (R_shell − r)
- For r ≪ R_shell: the flux is approximately uniform
  (we are deep inside the shell — the geometry is nearly flat)
- The GRADIENT of the flux — how it changes with position —
  depends on the local matter distribution (occlusion)

The pressure drops with distance from the Clearing boundary.
This is NOT because the boundary is receding — it's because
the throughput weakens with geometric spreading.

Light traversing this weakening pressure converts amplitude
to wavelength. This is the redshift. The conversion rate is
proportional to the local pressure gradient (κ = H₀/c at the
present epoch).

## 3. The Compounding Problem of Metric Expansion

### 3.1 How expansion compounds

In metric expansion, at time t, a volume V of space has a
scale factor a(t). At time t + dt, the scale factor is
a(t + dt) = a(t) + ȧ dt.

The NEW volume is V' = V × (a(t+dt)/a(t))³.

At the NEXT timestep, V' is the starting volume. The
expansion applies to V', not to V. Every timestep resets
the volumetric basis.

This is exponential in the Λ-dominated era (de Sitter):
```
a(t) ∝ exp(H₀ t)
```

And power-law in the matter-dominated era:
```
a(t) ∝ t^(2/3)
```

Both are compounding. The distance to the CMB at each moment
is a(t) × d_comoving, where d_comoving is fixed but a(t)
grows without bound.

### 3.2 The CMB should be unreachable

If the space at the CMB boundary is also expanding:
- The CMB photons emitted at z = 1100 are being carried away
  by the expansion of the space they occupy
- The space between those photons and us is also expanding
- The space that WAS between us has expanded, and the new
  space is now ALSO expanding
- Each layer of expansion creates new distance that itself
  expands

The standard model handles this by saying: the photons were
emitted when the universe was small (a = 1/1101). They have
been traveling through expanding space for 13.8 Gyr. The
expansion behind them has stretched the space they already
crossed, which is why the comoving distance (46.3 Gly) is
larger than the light-travel distance (13.8 Gly).

But this requires that the expansion was SLOWER than light
for the entire journey. If at any point the expansion rate
at the photon's current position exceeded c, the photon
would be moving backward (the space ahead of it expanding
faster than it can cross). This is the horizon problem.

The Hubble radius (c/H₀ ≈ 14.4 Gly) defines where v_rec = c.
Objects beyond this distance are "receding faster than light."
CMB photons were emitted from regions that are NOW at 46.3 Gly
— far beyond the Hubble radius. The standard explanation: they
were inside the Hubble radius when emitted, and the Hubble
radius has grown since.

This is internally consistent within ΛCDM. The maths works.
But the physical picture is: photons surfed the expansion —
they were emitted from "inside" the horizon, the horizon grew
around them, and they arrived. This requires exquisitely
fine-tuned initial conditions and expansion history.

### 3.3 The co-moving CMB paradox

If the CMB boundary is co-moving (expanding with space), then:
- The space at the boundary has expanded by a factor of 1101
  since emission
- The photons have been diluted by (1101)³ in number density
- Their wavelengths have been stretched by 1101

All correct by ΛCDM. But:

The boundary itself was a physical surface — the last
scattering surface. It had a temperature (3000 K), a density,
a physical extent. If the space AT that surface expanded
within itself, then the surface itself expanded. The atoms on
that surface moved apart. The plasma thinned out.

In the standard model, this is what happened — the plasma
DID thin out, the atoms DID move apart, and the photons DID
free-stream. This is recombination. The surface isn't a fixed
boundary — it's a moment in time when the whole universe
simultaneously became transparent.

But then: every point in space was simultaneously a CMB
emitter. The CMB isn't coming from a distant surface — it's
coming from everywhere, including from where we are now, at
an earlier time. The photons we detect were emitted from matter
that is now 46.3 Gly away, but was "right here" (in co-moving
coordinates) at the time.

This raises the question: if the CMB is from "right here" at
an earlier time, and "right here" has been expanding since
then, why is the CMB so uniform? The expansion should have
created density variations, shear, anisotropies. The standard
model explains the uniformity via inflation (a period of
exponential expansion that smoothed everything out before
recombination). This is a fix for a problem created by the
expansion model itself.

### 3.4 Without expansion

## 4. What the Redshift Actually Is

The redshift is the COST OF TRANSIT. Light traversing the
pressure gradient pays for its passage by converting amplitude
to wavelength. The conversion rate depends on the local
gradient strength (κ).

The gradient exists because the throughput is geometric — it
spreads from a spherical shell. The throughput is CONSUMED by
matter (used to persist). So:
1. Throughput spreads → pressure drops with depth → gradient
2. Matter consumes throughput → pressure drops near matter → occlusion
3. Light traverses the gradient → amplitude converts → redshift
4. More gradient = more conversion = more redshift = "further away"

The Hubble "constant" H₀ = c × κ is the local conversion rate.
It measures the gradient, not an expansion rate.

## 5. Why Different Methods Get Different H₀

Each method samples a different part of the gradient:

| Method | Samples | κ value | H₀ |
|--------|---------|---------|-----|
| Planck (CMB) | Full sky, all depths | Global mean | 67.4 |
| TRGB (Freedman) | Extended local volume | Intermediate | 70.0 |
| Lensing (TDCOSMO) | Paths through galaxies | Moderate occlusion | 71.6 |
| Cepheids (SH0ES) | Local ~300 Mpc | Local gradient | 73.0 |

The ordering tracks matter density along the measurement path.
Less matter = less occlusion = more throughput reaching each
point = steeper gradient = higher κ = higher "H₀."

This is not a tension. It is the expected variation in a
non-uniform medium.
