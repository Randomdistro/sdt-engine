## 1. The Question

Can EC-capable elements form stable negative ions (anions)?

A negative ion means adding ONE extra electron beyond the neutral atom.
In SDT terms: can the convergence field accommodate an additional gear
beyond the closed configuration?

## 3. The Pattern

### 3.1 Who accepts, who rejects?

**REJECTERS** (7 of 9 closures): Be, Ar, Kr, Xe, Cd, Yb, Hg.
All have EA ≤ 0. Their closed configurations REFUSE an extra electron.

**ACCEPTERS** (2 of 9): Ca (+0.025 eV), Ba (+0.145 eV).
Both are alkaline earths (s² closures). Both can place the extra
electron in an empty p orbital: Ca (4p¹), Ba (6p¹).

### 3.2 Why Ca and Ba are different

Ca and Ba are s² closures. The s² pair completes the s-subshell,
but the p-subshell at the same principal quantum level is EMPTY.
An extra electron can slot into this empty p gear position.

Contrast with Be: also s², but at Z=4 the 2p orbital is so close
in energy to 2s that the closed 2s² pair provides no gap for p
attachment. The convergence field is too shallow.

Contrast with noble gases: p⁶ closures have NO empty orbital at
the same energy level. The next available position is the (n+1)s,
which is a new shell entirely — too far from the convergence field
to bind.

### 3.3 SDT grammar interpretation

Anion = adding one extra gear OUTSIDE the closed gear train.
- Noble gas closures: the gear train is COMPLETELY enclosed. No
  socket for a new gear. Anion impossible.
- s²-closures with empty p: one socket remains in the same shell.
  A gear can weakly attach (Ca: 0.025 eV, barely).
- d¹⁰/f¹⁴ closures: the d/f subshell is full AND the next s²
  is also full. Double closure = no socket.

## 4. The Ca⁻ Anomaly

Ca⁻ has EA = +0.02455 eV = **24.55 meV**.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| EA | 24.55 meV |
| Thermal energy at 300K | 25.9 meV |
| EA / k_B T at 300K | **0.95** |
| Stability at room temp | **UNSTABLE** (thermal detachment) |
| Stability at 27K | Marginally stable |
| Config | [Ar] 4s² 4p¹ |
| Isoelectronic with | Sc⁰ (scandium neutral) |

Ca⁻ is destroyed by room temperature thermal noise. It can only
exist in cold environments or in the gas phase at low temperatures.

### Relationship to Ca-41 EC

Ca-41 has Q_EC = 421 keV and t½ = 99,400 years. If Ca⁻ were used
as the EC source (i.e., Ca-41⁻ with 21 electrons), what changes?

The extra electron in 4p¹ is far from the nucleus (r ≈ 195 pm).
Its wavefunction density at the nuclear surface is negligible.
It would contribute essentially ZERO to the EC rate — the K-shell
(1s) electrons dominate. Ca-41⁻ would have the same EC half-life
as Ca-41⁰ to within measurement precision.

**Prediction**: Ca⁻ does not alter EC rate. The anion electron
is irrelevant to the nuclear gear train.

## 5. The Ba⁻ Case

Ba⁻ has EA = +0.14462 eV = **144.6 meV** (6× stronger than Ca⁻).

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| EA | 144.6 meV |
| Thermal energy at 300K | 25.9 meV |
| EA / k_B T at 300K | **5.58** |
| Stability at room temp | **STABLE** (not thermally detached) |
| Config | [Xe] 6s² 6p¹ |
| Isoelectronic with | La⁰ (lanthanum neutral) |

Ba⁻ is stable at room temperature — a genuinely bound anion.
This makes Ba the ONLY EC closure element with a robust anion.

### Ba-133⁻ and EC

Ba-133 has Q_EC = 517 keV and t½ = 10.551 years. Adding a 6p¹
electron creates Ba-133⁻ (57 electrons). The extra electron
is at r ≈ 253 pm, far from the nucleus. EC rate unaffected.

## 6. Double Anions (A²⁻)

Can ANY EC element form a dianion (two extra electrons)?

| Element | EA₂ | Dianion? |
|---------|-----|---------|
| Be | — | Absolutely not |
| Ca | −2.5 eV (est.) | No |
| Ba | −0.5 eV (est.) | No |
| All noble gases | — | No |
| Cd, Hg, Yb | — | No |

**No EC closure element can form a stable dianion.** The convergence
field accommodates at most ONE extra gear (Ca, Ba) and only just.
Two gears beyond closure is universally destructive.

## 7. The Anti-Anion Rule for Noble Gas Closures

**No noble gas (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn) forms a stable anion.**

This is absolute. EA is negative for ALL noble gases. The p⁶ closure
is a perfect gear train with no attachment points.

In SDT terms: the 6-gear p-shell is a complete hexagonal close-packing.
Every possible gear position is occupied. Adding a 7th gear has no
stable socket — it would have to start a new shell, which is too far
from the convergence field to bind.

### Contrast with halogens (one gear short of closure)

| Group | EA (eV) | Config | Pattern |
|-------|---------|--------|---------|
| F (Z=9) | +3.401 | ...2p⁵ → 2p⁶ | ONE gear SHORT → strong attachment |
| Cl (Z=17) | +3.613 | ...3p⁵ → 3p⁶ | ONE gear SHORT → strong attachment |
| Br (Z=35) | +3.364 | ...4p⁵ → 4p⁶ | ONE gear SHORT → strong attachment |
| Ne (Z=10) | < 0 | ...2p⁶ → 3s¹ | FULL → no attachment |
| Ar (Z=18) | < 0 | ...3p⁶ → 4s¹ | FULL → no attachment |
| Kr (Z=36) | < 0 | ...4p⁶ → 5s¹ | FULL → no attachment |

Halogens (one gear short of closure) have the HIGHEST electron
affinities in nature (3.3–3.6 eV). Noble gases (at closure) have
ZERO. The closure boundary is the sharpest transition in the periodic
table.
