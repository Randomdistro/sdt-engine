## 2. Beryllium (Z=4) — The Anomalous Metal

### 2.1 Beryllium's chemical identity crisis

Be is classified as an alkaline earth metal (Group 2) but behaves
MORE like aluminium (Group 13). This is the **diagonal relationship**:
Be↔Al share:
- Similar electronegativity (Be: 1.57, Al: 1.61)
- Amphoteric oxides (BeO, Al₂O₃)
- Strong tendency toward covalent bonding
- Formation of polymeric structures (BeCl₂ chains, AlCl₃ dimers)

**SDT explanation**: Be's 2s² closure is the SMALLEST possible
s-pair beyond helium. The convergence field at Z=4 is too shallow
to support purely ionic bonding. The 2s² gear pair is too close
to the nucleus to share electrons freely — it CLINGS, forming
directional covalent bonds like a p-block element.

### 2.2 Key compounds

| Compound | Structure | Anomaly | Properties |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **BeH₂** | Linear (gas), polymeric chains (solid) | Only hydride that polymerises | Covalent bridging H atoms |
| **BeCl₂** | Linear (gas), polymer chains (solid) | Bridged Cl, like metal halide | Melts at 399°C |
| **BeO** | Wurtzite (hexagonal) | Highest melting oxide (2507°C) | Extremely hard |
| **Be(C₅H₅)₂** | **Slipped sandwich** (η⁵/η¹) | UNIQUE among metallocenes | Asymmetric, dipolar |
| **Be₃Al₂(SiO₃)₆** | Beryl (emerald, aquamarine) | Hexagonal channel crystal | Gemstones |
| **Be₁₂Ti** | Intermetallic | Used in nuclear reactors | Neutron multiplier |

### 2.3 Beryllocene — the broken sandwich

Ferrocene Fe(C₅H₅)₂ is symmetric: both C₅H₅ rings bind equally (η⁵/η⁵).
Beryllocene Be(C₅H₅)₂ is ASYMMETRIC: one ring binds tightly (η⁵),
the other slips to a single carbon (η¹).

**SDT interpretation**: Be's 2s² convergence field cannot project the
uniform d-orbital symmetry needed for a symmetric sandwich. The 2s²
pair is directional, not spherically symmetric. It can only grip one
ring fully and weakly touch the other.

### 2.4 Magnetic properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Magnetic susceptibility | −9.0 × 10⁻⁶ cm³/mol |
| Type | **Diamagnetic** |
| Number unpaired electrons | **0** |

Be is diamagnetic. All electrons are paired (2s²). No paramagnetic
anomaly. The Be-7 nucleus (Jπ = 3/2⁻) has a nuclear magnetic moment,
but this is ~10⁶× weaker than electronic magnetism.

## 3. Noble Gas Compounds — The Impossible Chemistry

### 3.1 Argon: HArF (the first argon molecule)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Discovery | 2000 (Helsinki, Finland) |
| Method | UV photolysis of HF in solid Ar matrix at 8K |
| Structure | Linear H–Ar–F |
| Stability | Decomposes above **27 K** |
| Bond type | Weakly covalent Ar–H, ionic Ar⁺–F⁻ character |

HArF exists ONLY in a cryogenic argon matrix. It cannot be isolated
as a free molecule. The Ar–H bond is an "anti-bond" — it exists
because the F atom stabilises the Ar⁺H⁻ charge transfer.

**SDT interpretation**: The 3p⁶ closure is nearly impenetrable.
HArF forces the closure to partially open (one p electron shifts
to bonding). At 27 K this is barely viable; above 27 K the gear
train snaps back to the closed configuration and ejects the H atom.

### 3.2 Krypton: KrF₂

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Structure | Linear F–Kr–F |
| Stability | Decomposes at room temperature (slowly) |
| Kr–F bond | 188.9 pm (longer than covalent radii sum) |
| Use | Extremely powerful oxidising agent |

KrF₂ is more stable than HArF but still thermally fragile.
The 4p⁶ closure is slightly easier to breach than 3p⁶.

### 3.3 Xenon: XeF₂, XeF₄, XeF₆, XeO₃

| Compound | Geometry | Stability | Melting point |
|----------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| XeF₂ | Linear | **Room-temp stable** | 129°C |
| XeF₄ | Square planar | Room-temp stable | 117°C |
| XeF₆ | Distorted octahedral | Stable | 49°C |
| XeO₃ | Pyramidal | **Explosive** | decomposes |
| XePtF₆ | Ionic complex | First noble gas compound (1962) | — |

Xenon has the RICHEST chemistry of any noble gas. The 5p⁶ closure
can be breached by fluorine (the most electronegative element)
because:
1. Xe is large (r ≈ 108 pm) → orbital overlap with F is effective
2. The 5p electron is relatively weakly bound (IE₁ = 12.13 eV)
3. F's extreme electronegativity (3.98) pulls electron density from Xe

**SDT interpretation**: The 5p⁶ closure is the "loosest" p⁶ closure
(lowest IE₁ of any noble gas except Rn). The gear rigidity ρ = 0.685
is the lowest of the noble gas p⁶ closures. This allows F to pry
gears out of the closed train.

## 5. Mercury — The Liquid Metal

### 5.1 Why mercury is liquid

Hg (Z=80, [Xe]4f¹⁴5d¹⁰6s²) is the only elemental metal that is
liquid at room temperature (mp = −38.83°C).

**SDT explanation**: The 6s² pair is relativistically contracted
(velocity v ≈ 0.58c for inner electrons). This contraction makes
the 6s² pair inert — too tightly bound to share in metallic bonding.
Without s-electron delocalisation, Hg atoms interact only via weak
van der Waals forces, giving a low melting point.

### 5.2 Unique compounds

| Compound | Anomaly |

| HgCl₂ (calomel) | Linear molecular crystal |
| CH₃HgCl (methylmercury) | Neurotoxin; bioaccumulates |
| Hg(OAc)₂ | Oxymercuration reagent |

Like Be, Hg is diamagnetic (all electrons paired). The 5d¹⁰6s²
closure has zero unpaired electrons.

## 6. Ytterbium — The Anomalous Lanthanide

### 6.1 Why Yb is anomalous

Yb (Z=70, [Xe]4f¹⁴6s²) behaves more like an alkaline earth than
a typical lanthanide:
- Forms stable Yb²⁺ (loses only 6s²) — unlike most Ln which are Ln³⁺
- Has anomalously low melting point for a lanthanide (824°C vs ~1000°C avg)
- Is soft, malleable, and sensitive to air

**SDT explanation**: The 4f¹⁴ closure is so stable that Yb prefers
to keep its 4f electrons and only give up the outer 6s² pair. Most
lanthanides give up 4f electrons because their f-shell is INCOMPLETE.
Yb's complete f-closure makes it chemically "inert below the surface."

### 6.2 Ytterbium in atomic clocks

Yb's ¹S₀ → ³P₀ clock transition at 578 nm is used in the most
precise optical atomic clocks. The narrow linewidth (millihertz)
comes from the fact that ³P₀ → ¹S₀ is STRICTLY forbidden (J=0→J=0).

**Relevance**: The clock transition's frequency stability is
sensitive to isotope shifts. ¹⁶⁹Yb (EC isotope) would have a
measurably different clock frequency from ¹⁷¹Yb or ¹⁷⁴Yb.

bluer (higher energy); larger dots emit redder.

**SDT interpretation**: The quantum dot size sets the convergence
field boundary conditions. The electron gear train is confined to
a finite space. The confinement changes the gear ratios, shifting
the emission wavelength. This is the macroscopic analogue of
changing the nuclear scaffold structure — changing the "box size"
