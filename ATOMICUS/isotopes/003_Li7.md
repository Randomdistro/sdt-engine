## Nuclear Construction Grammar

| Component | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Alpha core | 1 |
| Deuterons | 0 |
| Tritons | 1 |

$$A = 4 + 2(0) + 3(1) = 7 \quad Z = 2 + 0 + 1 = 3 \checkmark$$

Li-7 is alpha + one triton. The triton "caps" one face of the alpha tetrahedron, creating a prolate spheroid. Despite having an odd triton count (lower symmetry score = 59), Li-7 dominates over Li-6 (symmetry score = 100) at 92.41% vs 7.59%.

**Why the asymmetric isotope wins**: The triton's extra neutron provides additional neutron-bridge binding to the alpha core. The triton-alpha bond (Li-7: 39.2 MeV total, 5.61 MeV/nucleon) is stronger than the deuteron-alpha bond (Li-6: 32.0 MeV total, 5.33 MeV/nucleon). In SDT terms: the triton's extra neutron allows the internal electron to weave a more complex binding pattern with the alpha's nucleons, creating a deeper convergence well.

This establishes an important caveat to the symmetry rule: **for the lightest elements (Li, B), a single triton substitution can provide enough extra binding to overcome the symmetry advantage.** The symmetry rule becomes dominant only from carbon onward, where the number of deu/tri blocks is large enough for geometric arrangement to matter more than individual bond strength.

## The Primordial Lithium Problem

Standard cosmological models predict a primordial Li-7/H ratio of ~5 × 10⁻¹⁰, but observations of old, metal-poor stars show ~1.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ — a factor of ~3 discrepancy. This is the "cosmological lithium problem."

SDT resolution via dinosaur-cascade nucleosynthesis: if the pre-Clearing pressure regime produced different decay products (dinosaur particles that cascade-decayed into modern baryons), the initial Li-7 production need not match the standard BBN prediction. The observed Li-7 abundance may be the correct primordial value, with the standard prediction being an artefact of incorrect assumptions about the pre-Clearing particle content.
